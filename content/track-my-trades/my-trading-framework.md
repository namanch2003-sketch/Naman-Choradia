## Why a Framework Matters

Most traders fail not because they can't find good setups — they fail because they can't stay disciplined when a trade goes against them. A written framework is your commitment to your future self. It removes emotion from the equation and replaces it with process.

My framework has three layers: **Trade Selection**, **Risk Management**, and **Trade Review**.

---

## Layer 1: Trade Selection

Every trade must pass a 5-point checklist before I enter:

1. **Trend alignment**: Is the trade in the direction of the primary trend? I only trade counter-trend setups with reduced size.

2. **Setup quality**: Is there a clear technical trigger — a breakout, a bounce from support, or a momentum signal — with a well-defined entry point?

3. **Risk:Reward ratio**: Minimum 1:2 before entering. If the target is not at least twice the stop distance, I pass.

4. **Volume confirmation**: Is price action supported by volume? A breakout on low volume is suspect.

5. **Event calendar check**: Are there any earnings, result briefings, policy announcements, or expiry events within the trade's expected holding period?

If a trade fails even one of these checks, I do not enter — regardless of how compelling it looks.

---

## Layer 2: Risk Management

These are non-negotiable rules:

**Position sizing**:
- Risk per trade: Maximum 1% of total capital
- This means if I have ₹5,00,000 in my trading account, no single trade can risk more than ₹5,000
- Lot size is calculated accordingly: Position size = Risk Amount ÷ (Entry − Stop Loss)

**Stop losses**:
- Stop loss is set *before* entry, always
- Stop losses are placed at technically meaningful levels (below support, above resistance) — not arbitrary percentage-based stops
- I do not widen stops to "give the trade more room." If the stop is hit, the trade thesis has been invalidated.

**Maximum concurrent positions**: 3 positions at any time. More than this and I lose the ability to monitor each trade properly.

**Drawdown rule**: If I hit a 5% drawdown in a single week, I stop trading for the remainder of that week and review all recent trades.

---

## Layer 3: Trade Review

Every Sunday evening, I review all trades from the past week:

- What was the entry thesis? Was it correct?
- Did I follow my checklist? If not, why?
- What was the outcome? Was it luck or skill?
- What would I do differently?

I maintain a trade journal where every trade has: date, instrument, entry, stop, target, rationale, and outcome. Over time, this data reveals patterns — which setups work, which I should avoid, and where my discipline breaks down.

---

## Mindset Principles

**Accept losses as the cost of doing business.** A trader who refuses to take small losses will eventually take catastrophic ones.

**Don't attach your self-worth to a trade.** A losing trade does not make you a bad trader. Violating your rules does.

**Markets are probabilistic.** Even the best setup fails sometimes. Focus on executing process, not outcomes.

**Patience is an edge.** Most retail traders overtrade. Your best trades come from waiting for the highest-quality setups.

This framework is a living document. It will evolve as I learn more — but the core principles of defined risk, minimum 1:2 R:R, and disciplined review will never change.
